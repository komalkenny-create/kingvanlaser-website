Why UV for Polyamide Thermal Strips

Thermal barrier strips for aluminum windows are predominantly manufactured from glass-fiber-reinforced polyamide 66 (PA66 GF25), a material prized for its high mechanical strength, low thermal conductivity, and dimensional stability under temperature cycling. However, the same material properties that make PA66 GF25 an excellent thermal barrier also make it a difficult substrate for permanent marking.

Conventional fiber laser marking at 1064 nm infrared wavelength relies on thermal absorption to create a mark. When applied to PA66 GF25, this thermal absorption is uneven — the polyamide matrix and the glass fiber reinforcement absorb IR energy at different rates — leading to localized overheating, surface melting, discoloration, and in severe cases, micro-cracking around the glass fiber interfaces. These thermal defects can compromise the tensile strength of the strip, particularly at the narrow web sections that carry the structural load in the assembled window frame.

UV laser marking at 355 nm operates on a fundamentally different principle. At this wavelength, photon energy is high enough to directly break molecular bonds in the polymer matrix without significant thermal energy transfer — a process known as "cold ablation." The UV pulses remove material from the surface at the molecular level, creating a mark that is clean, high-contrast, and free of thermal damage to the surrounding material. For PA66 GF25, this means legible, durable marks without compromising the mechanical integrity of the strip.

Material Compatibility and Mark Quality

KingVan's UV laser marking systems produce three distinct mark types on PA66 GF25, selectable through parameter adjustment:

  • Light engraving (white mark): Achieved at lower fluence levels (1-3 J/cm²). UV laser pulses remove the surface layer of the polyamide matrix while leaving the embedded glass fibers exposed. The resulting mark appears as a light-colored contrast against the darker polyamide background. White marks provide the highest contrast for barcode scanning and are preferred for Data Matrix and QR code applications.
  • Dark engraving (black/charcoal mark): Achieved at moderate fluence levels (3-6 J/cm²) with a defocused beam. Modifies the surface carbonization level to produce a dark mark. Suitable for alphanumeric text and company logos where a more discreet or brand-consistent appearance is desired.
  • Deep engraving (grooved mark): Achieved at higher fluence and multiple passes (6-15 J/cm² cumulative). Physically removes 50-200 microns of material to create a tactile, recessed mark. Can be felt by touch and provides the highest durability in abrasive environments. Typically specified for serial numbers and traceability codes that must survive the entire product lifecycle including installation, use, and recycling.

In testing across 15 commercially available PA66 GF25 formulations from major European and Chinese compound suppliers, KingVan's UV marking systems produced consistent A-grade barcode readability (ISO/IEC 15415) with zero instances of surface charring or visible fiber protrusion when operated within the specified parameter range.

Production Speed Optimization

The primary speed constraint in UV laser marking of thermal strips is not the laser itself but the galvanometer scanner's ability to position the beam between marking fields. For strips with a total marking area of 20 mm by 10 mm (sufficient for a batch identifier, date code, and Data Matrix symbol), a typical marking cycle at resolutions of 0.05 mm consists of:

  • Two seconds for the laser to mark the 200 mm² area at 200 kHz repetition rate with the appropriate raster pattern
  • 0.3 seconds for the strip indexing mechanism to advance the next strip into position
  • 0.1 seconds for the vision system to verify mark quality

This yields a cycle time of approximately 2.4 seconds per strip, corresponding to a theoretical throughput of 25 strips per minute per laser head. In multi-head configurations (e.g., dual-lane systems), KingVan's KV-UV15 can achieve throughputs of 150 strips per minute, as validated in production at a major Chinese extrusion facility.

For operators seeking to optimize speed, the key parameters are repetition rate (higher = faster at reduced pulse energy), marking field size (larger = fewer positioning moves but reduced resolution), and the use of KingVan's advanced HSP (High-Speed Positioning) algorithm that pre-calculates the optimal scanning path to minimize galvanometer travel distance between marking vectors.

Mark Durability and Standards Compliance

Thermal barrier strips for certified window systems must meet the marking durability requirements of EN 14024:2020, which specifies minimum legibility after abrasive wear testing and accelerated weathering exposure. KingVan's UV laser marks are tested to the following protocol:

  • Abrasion resistance (EN 14024 Section 8.4): 5,000 cycles of abrasive pad rub testing with 500 g load. UV marks at light and moderate engraving depths showed zero measurable degradation in barcode scan rate. Deep engraving marks showed less than 2% reduction in contrast after the full test cycle.
  • UV weathering (ISO 4892-2): 1,000 hours of xenon-arc exposure simulating 5 years of outdoor service. Light and dark marks showed no measurable fading or discoloration. Deep engraving marks retained full legibility.
  • Thermal cycling (EN 14024 Annex B): 120 cycles from -20°C to +80°C with 4-hour dwell at each extreme. No observable change in mark quality or adhesion.
  • Chemical resistance (ISO 175): 24-hour immersion in 10% NaOH, 10% HCl, and isopropyl alcohol. All marks remained fully legible with less than 5% reduction in contrast.

These results confirm that UV laser marking on PA66 GF25 meets the most stringent durability requirements for certified thermal barrier window systems. The mark is effectively permanent for the service life of the window.

Implementation Considerations

Integrating UV laser marking into a thermal strip production line requires attention to a few practical factors. For extrusion lines running at linear speeds above 30 meters per minute, KingVan recommends a flying optics configuration where the laser marking head is mounted on a linear servo stage synchronized with the strip travel. At lower line speeds, a stop-and-mark configuration — where the strip is momentarily paused under the laser — offers lower capital cost and simpler installation.

Fume extraction is essential for UV laser marking of polyamide materials. The ablation process generates sub-micron particulates that must be captured at the source to prevent re-deposition on the optics and to maintain workplace air quality within permissible exposure limits. KingVan supplies integrated extraction nozzles with each marking system, configured for direct connection to the customer's existing extraction system or to KingVan's packaged filtration units.

KingVan provides on-site process development support for each installation, including parameter optimization for the specific polyamide formulation used by the customer and integration with the customer's existing ERP and traceability systems. For manufacturers running multiple strip profiles, the system's recipe management function stores marking parameters for up to 500 distinct product configurations, enabling instant changeover between production runs.
